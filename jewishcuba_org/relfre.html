Professor: Cubans Free To Worship

By GEORGE GEDDA
.c The Associated Press
June17, 1998

WASHINGTON (AP)- After long years of severe repression, the Cuban government now imposes no restrictions on freedom of worship, and religious freedoms have been broadened, a Cuban professor said Wednesday.

``One can't really speak of religious persecution in Cuba today,'' said Enrique Lopez Oliva, a professor at the University of Havana, who is on a tour of several U.S. cities to talk about the impact of Pope John Paul II's visit to Cuba in January.

Lopez Oliva, who co-edits an independent magazine, noted that during Holy Week in April, religious pilgrimages appeared in Cuba once again, ``without any difficulty or barrier.'' He added that he teaches at a Bible study group that attracts about 300 students each Saturday.

It is highly unusual for a Cuban who is not a spokesman for the government to talk about the situation on the island in a public session in the United States. Lopez Oliva said his request for permission to visit the United States was approved quickly by Cuban authorities. His Capitol Hill appearance Wednesday was sponsored by the Washington Office on Latin America, which monitors political and human rights issues in the hemisphere.

During the early days of the revolution, the Roman Catholic Church was seen as an enemy of the revolution, and several hundred priests were expelled. Cuba was officially atheist until 1992, when it became a secular state. Bars to Communist Party membership for religious believers were dropped.

The State Department's latest annual human rights report, published in January, said Cuba had eased the ``harsher aspects'' of religious repression but still imposed limits on access to the media and continued to ban the establishment of religious schools.

The report was completed before the pope's Jan. 21-25 visit to Cuba.

Over the years, Lopez Oliva had sought greater space for the church in Cuba. During the 1980s, he published a newsletter on religion in Cuba that was eventually suppressed by the regime.

He said Cuba has a number of independent religious publications, and they are not subject to prior censorship.

``I am co-editor of a magazine, and nobody reviews me before I get published,'' he said.

He acknowledged that one of the dangers the church faces is that it might be seen as a stalking horse for the counterrevolution.

To combat this perception, he said the church has to be seen as a ``respectable interlocutor - in other words, that it won't stab (President) Fidel (Castro) in the back.''

He added that the church must be seen as a possible ally of the regime as it copes with the transition brought about by the breakup of the Soviet bloc and attempts to deal with the new reality.

``Nonetheless, it cannot be denied that, although the church carries out a pastoral mission, its activities transcend the narrow religious framework and encompasses a political framework with respect to the national and social well-being,'' he said.
