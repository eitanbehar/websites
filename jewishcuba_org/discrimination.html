Israeli government discriminates against Cuba Jews - Editorial

On Thursday May 8 the following article by Amiram Barkat, Haaretz Correspondent and Haaretz Service, appeared in Haaretz.

Immigrant home-buying grants reinstated

The government will reinstate home-buying grants for immigrants from four countries - Argentina, Uruguay, France and South Africa - the state will tell the High Court of Justice Thursday morning.

Israel Radio reported Thursday, however, that the concession, which comes in response to a petition by the Jewish Agency and various immigrant associations against the cancellation of the grants on April 14, will only last for six months.

Until April 14, immigrants were entitled to both grants and loans when buying apartments, but on that date the grants were canceled and replaced by loans.

As a result, many immigrants' mortgage payments rose by dozens or even hundreds of percent. Immigrants from these four countries were particularly affected, because they are entitled to larger grants than all other immigrants except Ethiopians, for whom the government has already arranged compensation. (end of article)

I do not question the need for Israel to take stringent measures to improve its economy. I do not question the need to make an exception for Argentinian and Uruguayan Jews. Their countries are in desperate financial circumstances, and immigrants from these two countries have little support from home.

What I do question is the inclusion of South Africa and France, while Cuba is excluded. The reasons for this decision are unclear, but probably involve a lack of protexia by Cubans, and substantial political clout by the Jewish Agency on behalf of the others.

But why Cubans? Are they considered wealthy immigrants? The reality is that Cuban Jews arrive in Israel with approximately $20 given by the Jewish Agency, and a few clothes in a small bag. No containers, no money from sold properties, nothing.

South Africans? I've lived in South Africa. My oldest daughter is South African. Finding a poor Jew in South Africa is like finding a homeless person in Norway. They may be there, but no one remembers when they last saw one.

The French? To consider French Jewry as economically needy, and to say that French olim have no support from home, takes great imagination. Historically the French have demonstrated strong, supportive family affiliations, and France is not a poor country.

So why is the Israeli government discriminating against Cuban Jews? Why are Cuban Jews, with limited resources but great courage, not fully welcomed into Israeli society? Why does the government send them the message, "The French and South Africans, although more wealthy, need more help than you do?" Why does the government tell the Cubans, "We don't want you to buy a home?"

The Jewish olim from Cuba, who have lived for decades in an oppressive religious environment, deserve better treatment than this.

Richard Smith
May 12, 2003
