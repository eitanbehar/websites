ASSESSING THE RISKS

What happens if you break the law?

It is illegal for Americans to visit Cuba as tourists. Yet few people who do so wind up in trouble.

Last year, President Clinton signed the Helms-Burton Act, which raises the potential penalties on Americans who go to Cuba as tourists to $250,000 in criminal fines and $55,000 in civil fines. The Treasury Department has brought 10 criminal indictments for violations related to travel to Cuba in the past 15 years, and the possibility of civil fines is small but real. Penalties of $1,500 to $2,000 have been assessed in 44 civil cases since 1994.

Students, academics, missionaries, and travelers whose expenses are paid by a person not subject to U.S. jurisdiction are among those who can seek special Treasury Department approval for entering Cuba. For those in such categories, Marazul Tours (800-223-5334) can make hotel and airline reservations. If you don't qualify but decide to buck the sanctions anyway, travel agencies in Toronto, Cancun, and Nassau will book you on a tour. Most practice a "don't ask, don't tell'' policy regarding a customer's citizenship.

(From U.S. News & World Report)
